Spending on Science

U.S. federal agencies spend tens of billions of dollars on science and R&D each year. This spending follows a relatively predictable schedule, which ensures agencies are able to efficiently allocate resources to the most effective and meritorious research teams. This site follows both total obligation rates and new award activity at five major science agencies to transparently track the pace of spending on science.

What this tracks. Obligations are commitments to spend federal funds, and represent the broadest measure of agencies' spending activity. This tab shows total obligations within each agency's research function, including grants, contracts, salaries, and continuations. The data come from OMB's SF 133 Report on Budget Execution, and are normalized to the reported appropriations for the relevant agency functions. Because it captures all spending, the obligation pace is slow to reflect disruptions to new research funding specifically. See the New Awards tab for that view.

Each line shows how an agency's current obligation rate compares to its historical average. A value below 100% means the agency has obligated less than it typically would have by that point in the fiscal year.

Reading this chart: The 100% line is each agency's historical average pace. An agency at 80% has obligated only four-fifths of what it normally would have by this point in the year. The Oct–Nov segment is faded because October marks the start of the fiscal year, when all agencies begin on pace by definition.

What this tracks. The obligation data on the previous tab measure total spending commitments, including continuations, salaries, and contracts. This tab tracks the pace of new grant-making: how quickly agencies are issuing new awards compared to prior years. For NIH and NSF, data come from agency-specific APIs (NIH Reporter, NSF Awards). For DOE, NASA, and USDA, new award obligations are drawn from USASpending.gov. All sources are filtered to new awards only, excluding continuations and modifications. Comparisons in new grant spending are normalized to the reported appropriations for the relevant agency functions – rather than total dollar amounts, or fraction of end-of-year spending – to account for changes over time in the total agency budget and/or full-year underspending.

Each line shows an agency's current award-making pace as a percentage of its historical average at the same point in the fiscal year.

Reading this chart: The 100% line is each agency's historical average award-making pace. An agency at 50% has issued only half the new award dollars it normally would have by this point in the fiscal year, as a share of its annual appropriation. NIH and NSF values are based on individual awards from agency APIs; DOE, NASA, and USDA values are based on monthly new-award obligations from USASpending.gov.

Methodology. All figures are drawn from OMB SF-133 Reports on Budget Execution and Budgetary Resources, published by the Office of Management and Budget. These reports are generated from agency-reported data in the FACTS II system and represent the official record of federal budget execution.

Obligations are measured using Line 2190 (total new obligations & upward adjustments, combining unexpired and expired accounts). This is the broadest measure of binding spending commitments.

Appropriations use Line 1100 (discretionary) + Line 1200 (mandatory)—the raw enacted appropriation figures rather than net totals (Lines 1160/1260). Net totals include CR preclusions (Line 1134), which can dramatically understate the full-year appropriation during a Continuing Resolution. Once a full-year appropriations bill is enacted, Line 1134 goes to zero and the raw and net figures converge.

Reporting cadence. SF-133 data is reported monthly for October through September of each fiscal year. Quarterly reports (December, March, June, September) are considered official; monthly reports are preliminary. This tracker uses all available periods for the most current picture.

Agency filtering. Each tracked agency corresponds to specific Treasury Account Fund Symbols (TAFS) or bureau codes within department-level SF-133 files. For example, NIH data is filtered by bureau name within the HHS file, excluding management and facilities accounts that are not research-related. NSF, DOE Office of Science, NASA Science, and USDA research accounts are filtered by specific TRACCT codes.

Historical range. The shaded band on spend-down charts shows the minimum-to-maximum range across prior fiscal years (FY2016–FY2024). The dashed line shows the average. These provide context for whether current-year spending is within normal bounds.

Glossary

Appropriation
Budget authority enacted by Congress allowing an agency to incur obligations and make payments.
Obligation
A binding commitment to pay for goods, services, or other expenses—the primary measure of spending activity.
Outlay
Actual disbursement of funds from the Treasury. May lag behind obligations by months or years.
Budget Authority
Total authority to enter financial obligations, including appropriations, borrowing, and contract authority.
Continuing Resolution
Temporary funding at prior-year levels when full-year appropriations have not been enacted. During a CR, Line 1134 preclusions reduce net appropriation figures substantially.
Methodology. New award data tracks the pace of grant-making—how many new grants are issued and how much new funding is committed—as distinct from total obligation figures, which include continuing grants, salaries, and contracts. Awards are drawn from agency-specific APIs and grant databases, filtered to new and competing-renewal grants, and normalized against discretionary appropriations to allow comparison across years and agencies.

NIH data comes from the NIH Reporter API. Awards are filtered to Type 1 (new) and Type 2 (competing renewal) grants, excluding subprojects. Date is based on the award notice date. Data is available daily from FY2016 onward.

NSF data comes from the NSF Awards API. All new awards are included, dated by the award decision date (not the project start date, which may be months later). Data is available daily from FY2016 onward.

DOE Office of Science, NASA Science, and USDA data comes from USASpending.gov, filtered to project grants and cooperative agreements (award type codes 04 and 05) under agency-specific CFDA program numbers. The new_awards_only filter excludes continuations and modifications. Data is reported monthly. USASpending coverage begins in FY2017–2019 depending on the agency; years with incomplete data are automatically excluded from the historical baseline.

Historical range. The shaded band on award charts shows the minimum-to-maximum range across available prior fiscal years. The dashed line shows the average. Because USASpending data availability varies by agency, the baseline period may differ: NIH and NSF use FY2016–2024, while DOE, NASA, and USDA baselines begin later.

Normalization. To compare across years and agencies, cumulative new award dollars are expressed as a percentage of the discretionary appropriation (SF-133 Line 1100) for the relevant research component. This removes variation from budget changes over time, isolating changes in award-making pace. The overview chart divides the current value by the historical average at the same point in the fiscal year to produce the "% of average pace" metric. Metric cards use the same normalization: year-over-year and vs-average comparisons are based on appropriation-normalized values, not raw dollar amounts. Agencies whose average appropriation share is below 0.05% at a given point are excluded from the overview chart to avoid noisy ratios from small denominators.

Glossary

New Award (NIH)
A Type 1 (new) or Type 2 (competing renewal) grant, as classified by NIH. Excludes non-competing continuations, supplements, and subprojects.
New Award (NSF)
An award whose decision date falls within the fiscal year. Continuing grant increments retain the original award's start date and are not double-counted.
New Grant Obligation (USASpending)
A project grant or cooperative agreement obligation classified as a new award by USASpending.gov, excluding modifications to existing awards.
Cumulative Dollars
Running total of new award dollars from the start of the fiscal year (October 1) through the latest available date.
% of Appropriation
Cumulative new award dollars divided by the agency's full-year discretionary appropriation (SF-133 Line 1100). Provides a budget-normalized view of award pace.
