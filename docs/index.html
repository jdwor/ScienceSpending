Tracking Science Spending

U.S. federal agencies spend tens of billions of dollars on science each year, often following a predictable pattern. This site monitors both total obligation rates and new award activity at five major science agencies to transparently track the pace of spending on science.

What this tracks. Obligations are commitments to spend federal funds, and represent the broadest measure of agencies' spending activity. This tab shows total obligations within each agency's research function, including grants, contracts, salaries, and continuations. The data come from OMB's SF 133 Report on Budget Execution, and are normalized to the reported appropriations for the relevant agency functions. Because it captures all spending, the obligation pace is slow to reflect disruptions to new research funding specifically. See the New Awards tab for that view.

Each line shows how an agency's current obligation rate compares to its historical average. A value of 0% means the agency is on pace; negative values mean it has obligated less than it typically would have by that point in the fiscal year.

Reading this chart: The 0% line means an agency is exactly on its historical pace. An agency at −20% has obligated 20% less than it normally would have by this point in the year. The dashed segment at the start of each line bridges from October, when the fiscal year begins and agencies are on pace by definition, to the first available data point.

What this tracks. The obligation data on the previous tab capture total spending commitments, including continuations, salaries, and contracts. This tab focuses on new grant-making. For NIH and NSF, data come from agency-specific APIs (NIH Reporter, NSF Awards). For DOE, NASA, and USDA, new award obligations are drawn from USASpending.gov. All sources are filtered to new awards only, excluding continuations and modifications. New grant spending is normalized to each agency's appropriation, rather than compared in raw dollar terms or as a fraction of end-of-year totals. This accounts for year-to-year changes in agency budgets and for years in which agencies do not fully spend their appropriation.

Reading this chart: The 0% line means an agency is exactly on its historical award-making pace. An agency at −50% has issued half the new award dollars it normally would have by this point in the fiscal year, as a share of its annual appropriation. The dashed segment at the start of each line bridges from October, when the fiscal year begins and agencies are on pace by definition, to the first available data point.

Export
Methodology. All figures are drawn from OMB SF-133 Reports on Budget Execution and Budgetary Resources, published by the Office of Management and Budget. These reports are generated from agency-reported data in the FACTS II system and represent the official record of federal budget execution.

Obligations are measured using Line 2190 (total new obligations & upward adjustments), filtered to unexpired accounts only. Expired-account adjustments (Line 2180) are excluded as they represent prior-year corrections rather than current-year spending activity.

Appropriations use Line 1100 (discretionary) + Line 1200 (mandatory)—the raw enacted appropriation figures rather than net totals (Lines 1160/1260). Net totals include CR preclusions (Line 1134), which can dramatically understate the full-year appropriation during a Continuing Resolution. Once a full-year appropriations bill is enacted, Line 1134 goes to zero and the raw and net figures converge.

Reporting cadence. SF-133 data is reported monthly for October through September of each fiscal year. Quarterly reports (December, March, June, September) are considered official; monthly reports are preliminary. This tracker uses all available periods for the most current picture.

Continuing Resolutions. During a Continuing Resolution, Line 1100 reflects the annualized prior-year rate. Obligation percentages may appear lower until a full-year bill is enacted, at which point Line 1134 preclusions go to zero and raw and net figures converge.

Glossary

Appropriation
Budget authority enacted by Congress allowing an agency to incur obligations and make payments.
Obligation
A binding commitment to pay for goods, services, or other expenses—the primary measure of spending activity.
Outlay
Actual disbursement of funds from the Treasury. May lag behind obligations by months or years.
Budget Authority
Total authority to enter financial obligations, including appropriations, borrowing, and contract authority.
Continuing Resolution
Temporary funding at prior-year levels when full-year appropriations have not been enacted. During a CR, Line 1134 preclusions reduce net appropriation figures substantially.
Methodology. New award data cover the pace of grant-making: how many new grants are issued and how much new funding is committed. This is distinct from total obligation figures, which include continuing grants, salaries, and contracts. Awards are drawn from agency-specific APIs and grant databases, filtered to new and competing-renewal grants, and normalized against discretionary appropriations to allow comparison across years and agencies.

NIH data comes from the NIH Reporter API. Awards are filtered to Type 1 (new) and Type 2 (competing renewal) extramural grants, excluding subprojects and intramural research (activity codes starting with Z). Date is based on the award notice date. Data is available daily from FY2016 onward.

NSF data comes from the NSF Awards API, covering Research & Related Activities, STEM Education, Technology Innovation & Partnerships, and Integrative Activities (CFDA programs 47.041, .049, .050, .070, .074, .075, .076, .083, .084). All new awards are included, dated by the award decision date (not the project start date, which may be months later). Dollar amounts represent the initial obligation recorded for each award, extracted from the per-year funding breakdown in the NSF API. For standard grants, this equals the full award value; for continuing grants, it is the first-year increment. A small proportion of awards (~1%, stable across all fiscal years) lack per-year obligation data and are included in award counts but excluded from dollar totals. This is likely a conservative choice: the alternative field available for these awards tended to overstate dollar amounts slightly more in earlier fiscal years, so including it would marginally inflate historical baselines relative to more recent years. Data is available daily from FY2016 onward.

DOE Office of Science, NASA Science, and USDA data comes from USASpending.gov, filtered to project grants and cooperative agreements (award type codes 04 and 05) under agency-specific CFDA program numbers. The new_awards_only filter excludes continuations and modifications. Data is reported monthly. USASpending coverage begins in FY2017–2019 depending on the agency; years with incomplete data are automatically excluded from the historical baseline.

Comparability. Each source is filtered to approximate new competitive grant activity, but the exact construct differs: NIH includes Type 1 (new) and Type 2 (competing renewal) grants from NIH Reporter, NSF includes awards by decision date from the NSF Awards API, and USASpending is filtered to new award obligations. These series are broadly comparable as measures of new competitive grant activity, but they are not identical administrative constructs.

Historical range. The shaded band on award charts shows the minimum-to-maximum range across available prior fiscal years. The dashed line shows the average. Because USASpending data availability varies by agency, the baseline period may differ: NIH and NSF use FY2016–2024, while DOE begins FY2019, USDA begins FY2018, and NASA begins FY2017. Years with incomplete source coverage are automatically excluded from the baseline.

Normalization. To compare across years and agencies, cumulative new award dollars are expressed as a percentage of the discretionary appropriation (SF-133 Line 1100) for the relevant research component. Mandatory appropriation (Line 1200) is excluded from the awards denominator because competitive grants are funded from discretionary accounts; mandatory funding supports specific earmarked programs. (The Obligations tab uses discretionary + mandatory as its denominator because it tracks all spending activity.) This removes variation from budget changes over time, isolating changes in award-making pace. The overview chart divides the current value by the historical average at the same point in the fiscal year to produce the “% of average pace” metric. Metric cards use the same normalization: year-over-year and vs-average comparisons are based on appropriation-normalized values, not raw dollar amounts. Agencies whose average appropriation share is below 0.05% at a given point are excluded from the overview chart to avoid noisy ratios from small denominators. Year-over-year and versus-average comparisons in the metric cards are expressed as percentage-point differences, while the overview chart expresses pace as a ratio relative to the historical average.

Update cadence. Obligations reflect monthly SF-133 reporting. Award data reflect source-specific agency feeds and may cover a more recent period. Where the latest source period is partial, the final point is shown provisionally.

Glossary

New Award (NIH)
A Type 1 (new) or Type 2 (competing renewal) grant, as classified by NIH. Excludes non-competing continuations, supplements, and subprojects.
New Award (NSF)
An award whose decision date falls within the fiscal year, deduplicated at the award level. Continuing grant increments are counted as separate awards only if they receive distinct award IDs from NSF.
New Grant Obligation (USASpending)
A project grant or cooperative agreement obligation classified as a new award by USASpending.gov, excluding modifications to existing awards.
Cumulative Dollars
Running total of new award dollars from the start of the fiscal year (October 1) through the latest available date.
% of Appropriation
Cumulative new award dollars divided by the agency's full-year discretionary appropriation (SF-133 Line 1100). Provides a budget-normalized view of award pace.

Dollar amounts may differ from the agency APIs due to differences in how transactions are aggregated.
