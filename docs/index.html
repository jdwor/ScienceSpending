Tracking Science Spending

U.S. federal agencies spend tens of billions of dollars on science each year, often following a predictable pattern. This site monitors both total obligation rates and new award activity at five major science agencies to transparently track the pace of spending on science.

At a glance. Current award-making and spending pace at five major federal science agencies, compared to historical averages. Click an agency's card for temporal trends; and view detailed data, graphs, and metrics in the outcome-specific tabs above.

New Awards — newly issued competitive grants and renewals, excluding continuations.

All Awards — total grant obligations, including continuations and modifications.

Obligations — all spending commitments: grants, contracts, salaries, and facilities.

Percentages reflect cumulative dollar spending as a share of annual appropriations, compared to the historical average at the same point in the fiscal year. The New Awards, All Awards, and Obligations tabs above provide additional detail, including raw dollar amounts, award counts, and agency-level data.

What this tracks. This tab measures total obligation activity: binding commitments to spend federal funds, encompassing grants, contracts, salaries, continuations, and all other spending within each agency's research function. This is the broadest measure of agency spending, capturing every category of expenditure, not just grants. The data come from OMB's SF-133 Report on Budget Execution, reported monthly for each fiscal year. Obligation spending is normalized to each agency's appropriation, rather than compared in raw dollar terms or as a fraction of end-of-year totals. This accounts for year-to-year changes in agency budgets and for years in which agencies do not fully spend their appropriation.

Each line shows how an agency's current obligation rate compares to its historical average. A value of 0% means the agency is on pace; negative values mean it has obligated less than it typically would have by that point in the fiscal year.

Reading this chart: The 0% line means an agency is exactly on its historical pace. An agency at −20% has obligated 20% less than it normally would have by this point in the year. The dashed segment at the start of each line bridges from October, when the fiscal year begins and agencies are on pace by definition, to the first available data point.

Methodology. All figures are drawn from OMB SF-133 Reports on Budget Execution and Budgetary Resources, published by the Office of Management and Budget. These reports are generated from agency-reported data in the FACTS II system and represent the official record of federal budget execution.

Obligations are measured using Line 2190 (total new obligations & upward adjustments), filtered to unexpired accounts only. Expired-account adjustments (Line 2180) are excluded as they represent prior-year corrections rather than current-year spending activity.

Appropriations use Line 1100 (discretionary) + Line 1200 (mandatory)—the raw enacted appropriation figures rather than net totals (Lines 1160/1260). Net totals include CR preclusions (Line 1134), which can dramatically understate the full-year appropriation during a Continuing Resolution. Once a full-year appropriations bill is enacted, Line 1134 goes to zero and the raw and net figures converge.

Reporting cadence. SF-133 data is reported monthly for October through September of each fiscal year. Quarterly reports (December, March, June, September) are considered official; monthly reports are preliminary. This tracker uses all available periods for the most current picture.

Continuing Resolutions. During a Continuing Resolution, Line 1100 reflects the annualized prior-year rate. Obligation percentages may appear lower until a full-year bill is enacted, at which point Line 1134 preclusions go to zero and raw and net figures converge.

Glossary

Appropriation
Budget authority enacted by Congress allowing an agency to incur obligations and make payments.
Obligation
A binding commitment to pay for goods, services, or other expenses—the primary measure of spending activity.
Outlay
Actual disbursement of funds from the Treasury. May lag behind obligations by months or years.
Budget Authority
Total authority to enter financial obligations, including appropriations, borrowing, and contract authority.
Continuing Resolution
Temporary funding at prior-year levels when full-year appropriations have not been enacted. During a CR, Line 1134 preclusions reduce net appropriation figures substantially.
Methodology. New award figures measure the pace of competitive grant-making: how many new grants are issued and how much new funding is committed each fiscal year. Data are drawn from agency-specific APIs and grant databases, filtered to new and competing-renewal grants, and normalized against discretionary appropriations to allow comparison across years and agencies.

Data sources. NIH and NSF data come from agency-specific APIs that provide daily, award-level records. DOE, NASA, and USDA data come from USASpending.gov, which provides monthly aggregates.

Sub-agency detail. The agency detail view offers sub-agency breakdowns. NIH data can be filtered by Institute/Center using the IC assignment from NIH Reporter. Dollar amounts. NIH dollar amounts are the award amount reported by NIH Reporter. NSF dollar amounts represent the initial-year obligation extracted from the per-year funding breakdown in the NSF API; for standard grants, this equals the full award value, and for continuing grants, it is the first-year increment. A small proportion of NSF awards (~1%, stable across fiscal years) lack per-year obligation data and are included in award counts but excluded from dollar totals. USASpending amounts are monthly aggregated obligation dollars. All dollar figures are nominal and not adjusted for inflation.

NSF obligation timing. NSF dollar amounts reflect the full fiscal-year obligation committed at the time of the award decision. For large cooperative agreements (e.g., research vessel operations), the committed amount may be substantially larger than the initial transaction recorded in federal payment systems, with the remainder processed over subsequent months. Year-end totals converge with USASpending transaction records, but intra-year pace may appear faster in directorate-level charts, particularly for Geosciences where annual fleet operations contracts can represent a substantial share of the directorate’s total.

Award counts. Individual award counts are available for NIH and NSF, which provide award-level records. USASpending reports monthly aggregates, so award counts are not available for DOE, NASA, or USDA.

Comparability. Each source approximates new competitive grant activity, but the administrative constructs differ. NIH distinguishes award types explicitly (Type 1, Type 2). NSF captures all awards by decision date. USASpending uses a new_awards_only filter that isolates initial obligations on newly created award records. These series are broadly comparable but not identical.

Historical range. The shaded band on charts shows the minimum-to-maximum range across available prior fiscal years; the dashed line shows the average. NIH and NSF baselines cover FY2016–2024. USASpending coverage begins later for some agencies: NASA from FY2017, USDA from FY2018, DOE from FY2019. Years with incomplete source coverage are automatically excluded. FY2025 and FY2026 are shown as individual lines and excluded from the historical band.

Normalization. Cumulative new award dollars are expressed as a percentage of the discretionary appropriation (SF-133 Line 1100) for the relevant research component. Mandatory appropriation (Line 1200) is excluded because competitive grants are funded from discretionary accounts. The overview chart divides the current value by the historical average at the same point in the fiscal year to produce the “vs. average pace” metric. Agencies whose average appropriation share is below 0.05% at a given point are excluded from the overview chart to avoid noisy ratios from small denominators.

Update cadence. NIH and NSF data are fetched from agency APIs; caches expire after 24 hours for the current fiscal year. USASpending data is also refreshed on a 24-hour cycle. Where the latest reporting period is partial, the final data point is shown provisionally.

Glossary

New Award (NIH)
A Type 1 (new) or Type 2 (competing renewal) grant, as classified by NIH. Excludes non-competing continuations, supplements, and subprojects.
New Award (NSF)
An award whose decision date falls within the fiscal year, deduplicated at the award level.
New Grant Obligation (USASpending)
A project grant or cooperative agreement obligation classified as a new award by USASpending.gov, excluding modifications to existing awards.
Cumulative Dollars
Running total of new award dollars from the start of the fiscal year (October 1) through the latest available date.
% of Appropriation
Cumulative new award dollars divided by the agency’s full-year discretionary appropriation (SF-133 Line 1100).
Methodology. All-awards figures measure total grant obligation activity: new awards, non-competing continuations, renewals, and modifications to existing awards. This captures the full scope of grant funding flowing to the research community in a given fiscal year.

Data sources. NIH uses the NIH Reporter API with award types 1 (new), 2 (competing renewal), and 5 (non-competing continuation), providing daily award-level records with individual award counts. NSF, DOE, NASA, and USDA use USASpending.gov with the action_date date type, which captures all grant obligations where the action (new award, continuation, modification, or renewal) occurred during the fiscal year. USASpending provides monthly aggregated obligation amounts; individual award counts are not available for these agencies.

Award types. USASpending queries are filtered to project grants (award type code 04) and cooperative agreements (award type code 05). Contracts, direct payments, and other non-grant instruments are excluded. NIH Reporter queries exclude intramural research (activity codes starting with Z) and subprojects.

Negative obligations. Because USASpending action_date captures all transaction types, including deobligations and downward adjustments on existing awards, cumulative totals may briefly go negative early in a fiscal year for some agencies. Charts floor these values at zero for readability; the underlying data retains the actual values.

Historical range. The shaded band on charts shows the minimum-to-maximum range across available prior fiscal years; the dashed line shows the average. NIH all-awards data begins in FY2017; FY2016 is excluded because Type 5 (non-competing continuation) records are significantly underreported in NIH Reporter for that year, unlike FY2017 onward which align closely with USASpending totals. USASpending coverage begins later for some agencies: NASA from FY2017, USDA from FY2018, DOE from FY2019. Years with incomplete source coverage are automatically excluded. FY2025 and FY2026 are shown as individual lines and excluded from the historical band.
